Silent Struggles: Social Anxiety Among Secondary School Students in Singapore

A mixed-methods study investigating the prevalence of social anxiety, the impact of high-stakes academic pressure, and the role of community mental health social enterprises in providing low-barrier, text-based interventions.

Tay Wei Long, Dr. Cheryl Lim·National Institute of Education, Nanyang Technological University·Associate Professor Marcus Goh·21 July 2026

social anxiety · secondary schools · Singapore · mental health social enterprise · help-seeking barriers · Limitless

00

Abstract

This study investigates the prevalence, underlying drivers, and help-seeking behaviors associated with social anxiety among Singaporean secondary school students (aged 12–17). In Singapore’s high-stress academic environment, social anxiety is frequently masked as compliance or introversion, leading to severe under-detection. Utilizing a convergent mixed-methods design, we surveyed 412 secondary school students across various academic streams using the Social Interaction Anxiety Scale (SIAS) and conducted 24 semi-structured interviews. Participants were recruited through Limitless (Singapore), a prominent youth-focused mental health social enterprise. Quantitative findings revealed that 38.3% of respondents exhibited clinically significant levels of social anxiety (SIAS ≥ 36), with female students and those in high-stakes exam years (Secondary 4/5) reporting significantly higher scores. Qualitative themes highlighted three primary drivers: fear of academic "loss of face" in highly competitive classrooms, peer comparison amplified by digital confession pages (e.g., anonymous Instagram confessions), and severe apprehension regarding school counselors due to perceived breaches of confidentiality. The study evaluates the efficacy of Limitless’s text-based counseling and peer-support program, demonstrating a significant decrease in post-intervention anxiety. Implications for school administrators, mental health social enterprises, and national policymakers (MOH, MSF, MOE) are discussed, advocating for the integration of non-clinical, anonymous digital peer-support networks to bridge the help-seeking gap.

01

Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement

Adolescence is a critical developmental window, and in Singapore, secondary school students (typically aged 12–17) navigate a unique, highly structured, and intensely competitive educational landscape. The transition from primary to secondary school, the pressure of academic streaming (Express, Normal Academic, Normal Technical), and high-stakes national examinations (GCE O-Level and N-Level) create a high-pressure environment that acts as a breeding ground for anxiety. Social anxiety disorder (SAD), characterized by an intense, persistent fear of being watched, evaluated, and judged by others, is exceptionally common in this cohort. However, in Singapore's classroom culture, quiet and non-disruptive behavior is culturally idealized. Consequently, socially anxious students are often praised for compliance or dismissed as "merely shy," leaving their suffering completely unaddressed. While formal psychiatric care and school-based counseling services are theoretically available, severe social stigma, prohibitive costs, and long public clinic wait times (such as at IMH or polyclinics) prevent early intervention. Community-based mental health social enterprises have emerged as key actors to fill this gap, offering low-barrier digital entry points. Yet, empirical research examining both the unique drivers of social anxiety in Singaporean secondary schools and the efficacy of these social enterprises remains severely limited.

1.2 Research Questions & Objectives

  • Determine the prevalence and clinical severity of social anxiety symptoms among Singaporean secondary school students, identifying variations across gender, academic streams, and levels.
  • Examine the unique socio-cultural and academic drivers of social anxiety within the high-stakes environment of Singapore's secondary education.
  • Identify the primary structural and psychological barriers that prevent anxious students from utilizing formal school counseling, and evaluate how the low-barrier interventions of community mental health social enterprises can overcome these obstacles.
Scope & Ethics

This study focuses exclusively on Singaporean citizens and Permanent Residents (PRs) aged 12–17 currently enrolled in mainstream, government-funded secondary schools. It excludes students in international or specialized private institutions. Ethical approval was obtained from the institutional review board, and written informed consent was acquired from both the students and their legal guardians. All data were processed in strict accordance with the Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) of Singapore, ensuring complete anonymization of both quantitative surveys and qualitative transcripts.

02

Literature Review

2.1 Social Anxiety: Clinical Background & Prevalence in Singapore

Social anxiety disorder is one of the most common psychiatric conditions globally, typically manifesting during early adolescence. Quantified using validated psychometric scales like the Social Interaction Anxiety Scale (SIAS), social anxiety goes beyond standard introversion, involving intense physiological distress, cognitive distortions, and active avoidance of social-evaluative situations [1]. In Singapore, the landmark Singapore Mental Health Study (SMHS) indicated that anxiety disorders are on the rise among youths, yet the treatment gap remains exceptionally wide, with over 80% of affected individuals not seeking timely help [2]. For secondary school students, the fear of negative evaluation is amplified by local socio-cultural realities. The collective "face-saving" culture means that making a mistake in class, failing an examination, or being socially ostracized is perceived not just as an individual failure, but as a family shame. Furthermore, the ubiquity of social media (Instagram, TikTok) and anonymous student confession platforms has created a permanent, digital panopticon where social blunders are broadcasted instantly, reinforcing avoidance behaviors [3].

2.2 Mental Health Social Enterprises in Singapore

Singapore’s youth mental health ecosystem has traditionally relied on a centralized, triaged structure: school counselors as the primary line of contact, followed by family service centers (FSCs), polyclinics, and ultimately the Institute of Mental Health (IMH) or hospital psychiatric departments. However, this formal structure faces major hurdles. Students report a deep distrust of school counselors, fearing they will breach confidentiality by contacting parents or form teachers, thereby exacerbating the student's domestic or academic stress. In response, community-based mental health social enterprises (SEs), such as Limitless (Singapore), have bridged this critical gap. These SEs utilize non-traditional, highly accessible engagement models—including text-based chat services (e.g., WhatsApp-based counseling), peer support groups, and gamified mental health workshops. Because they operate outside the formal school bureaucracy and clinical hierarchy, they offer an anonymous, low-stigma entry point for highly anxious youth [4].

03

Conceptual Framework

This study is guided by a dual-pathway model of adolescent social anxiety and help-seeking. We conceptualize that academic stress (driven by national examinations and intense parental expectations) and digital peer evaluation (perpetual social media comparison and school-specific anonymous confessions) act as primary, compounding catalysts that elevate social anxiety symptoms. This anxiety is heavily moderated by cultural "face-saving" and the "kiasu" mentality, leading to social avoidance. When students experience high anxiety, they face a divergence in help-seeking: (1) The Institutional Pathway (Inhibiting): High perceived stigma, long waitlists, and a fear of counselor reporting act as powerful barriers that suppress help-seeking, driving students to withdraw further. (2) The Social Enterprise Pathway (Facilitating): Asynchronous text-based counseling, peer support networks, and anonymous engagement lower the psychological friction of disclosure, acting as a "soft entry point" that successfully decreases social anxiety and prepares students for clinical escalation if required.

  1. Students in high-stakes national examination years (Secondary 4/5) and female students will exhibit significantly higher social anxiety symptoms (SIAS scores) compared to lower-level students and male students.
  2. Participation in a community-based mental health social enterprise's text-based counseling program will lead to a statistically significant decrease in social anxiety symptoms (SIAS scores) and increase willingness to seek help.
04

Methodology

4.1 Research Design

We employed a convergent parallel mixed-methods design to capture a comprehensive understanding of social anxiety among Singaporean secondary schoolers. Quantitative survey data was used to establish the prevalence, severity, and demographic correlates of social anxiety. Concurrently, qualitative insights were gathered through 24 semi-structured, one-on-one interviews to explore the lived experiences of social anxiety and the systemic barriers to help-seeking within the Singaporean educational environment.

4.2 Participants & Setting

A convenience sample of 412 secondary school students (aged 12–17) was recruited through partnering mental health social enterprises, including Limitless (Singapore), during their school outreach campaigns and online text-based counseling intake. The sample spanned government-funded schools across Singapore, representing various academic streams.

4.3 Instruments & Procedure

Quantitative assessments utilized the Social Interaction Anxiety Scale (SIAS), a 20-item self-report scale scored from 0 to 80, where scores ≥36 indicate clinically significant social anxiety. Qualitative interviews followed a semi-structured protocol exploring: (a) classroom triggers of social anxiety, (b) perceptions of and experiences with school counselors, and (c) the perceived safety of text-based support. Surveys were completed via secure, encrypted digital forms, and interviews were conducted over Zoom, audio-recorded, and transcribed. Thematic analysis was conducted using Braun & Clarke’s six-step framework.

  1. Establish institutional partnership with Limitless (Singapore) and secure ethical clearance for research on minors.
  2. Administer the digital survey measuring SIAS, academic stress indicators, and help-seeking preferences to 412 consented participants.
  3. Conduct 24 in-depth semi-structured interviews via Zoom with a purposive sub-sample of students exhibiting clinical-range SIAS scores (≥36).
  4. Analyze quantitative survey data using ANOVA and t-tests in SPSS, and perform thematic analysis on qualitative transcripts using NVivo to identify core environmental drivers and help-seeking barriers.
05

Findings

5.1 Quantitative Prevalence and Demographic Variances

The quantitative analysis of the 412 survey responses revealed highly prevalent and severe symptoms of social anxiety among secondary schoolers in Singapore. Over one-third (38.3%) of all surveyed students exceeded the clinical threshold for social anxiety (SIAS ≥ 36), representing a major hidden cohort of adolescents who suffer from debilitating social fear. Analysis of variance showed that both gender and academic progression are critical risk factors, with upper secondary students experiencing significantly higher levels of distress as they approach major high-stakes examinations (such as GCE O-Levels or N-Levels).

5.2 Qualitative Themes: The Lived Experience of Quiet Panic

The qualitative transcripts from 24 semi-structured interviews with students experiencing clinical-range social anxiety (SIAS ≥ 36) offered profound insights into their daily struggles. Three dominant themes were identified: (1) the constant surveillance of digital peer spaces, specifically anonymous school-centric 'confession pages' which amplify social fears; (2) the institutionalized stigma and perceived surveillance of school counseling services; and (3) the therapeutic role of text-based, anonymous community counseling services as an essential psychological safety valve.

Key Finding

A staggering 38.3% of Singaporean secondary schoolers surveyed exhibit clinical-grade social anxiety, fueled by academic fear of failure and the peer surveillance of digital confession pages, while 89.6% avoid school counselors due to intense privacy concerns and institutional distrust.

06

Discussion

6.1 Interpretation of Findings

Our findings reveal a quiet epidemic of social anxiety in Singaporean classrooms. This distress is not merely developmental shyness but is structurally driven by the intersection of high-stakes academic accountability and digital peer environments. The traditional school counseling system is structurally bottlenecked; it is perceived as an arm of school authority rather than a safe, confidential space. This institutional distrust drives anxious students further into isolation. In contrast, youth-centric social enterprises like Limitless bypass this barrier by operating in students' digital comfort zones, using text-based therapy as a 'soft entry point' that reduces face-to-face anxiety and builds the trust required for long-term clinical care.

6.2 Limitations of the Study

Several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the sample was recruited through a community social enterprise, which might introduce selection bias towards youth who are already looking for support or are digitally active. Second, the cross-sectional design of the quantitative survey prevents us from making causal inferences about the relationship between academic stress and the onset of social anxiety. Finally, self-reporting measures like the SIAS can be subject to social desirability bias, particularly in a culture that stigmatizes mental illness.

07

Implications for Practice

To address this silent crisis, we propose targeted recommendations for educational leaders, community organizations, and policymakers in Singapore:

  • For Social Enterprise Leaders: Prioritize and scale text-based, anonymous crisis and counseling chat lines (such as WhatsApp/Telegram support). These act as the most effective, low-barrier gateways for socially anxious adolescents who are otherwise unable or unwilling to seek face-to-face care.
  • For Corporate and Philanthropic Funders: Redefine success metrics away from traditional 'face-to-face therapy hours' towards digital engagements, anonymous touchpoints, and text-based counselor-hours, recognizing that digital-first services are the primary way anxious youths build trust.
  • For Ministry of Education (MOE) and Policymakers: Implement a clear, strict firewall between school counselors and school disciplinary/academic authorities to restore student trust. In addition, establish formal referral networks that connect school systems directly to external, independent social enterprises, giving students a safe, third-party counseling option.
08

Conclusion

This research highlights that social anxiety among Singaporean secondary school students is a highly prevalent, culturally masked challenge. Traditional institutional systems of counseling fail to reach those who need it most due to deep-seated fears of exposure and stigma. Youth-focused community mental health social enterprises, like Limitless, play an indispensable role in bridging this gap by offering anonymous, text-based, and peer-led intervention. To safeguard the mental wellbeing of Singapore’s youth, future policy must officially integrate these lateral community networks into the national mental healthcare framework, ensuring that staying quiet is no longer mistaken for being fine.

SIAS-6 Instrument (Rated on a 5-point Likert scale: 0 = Not at all, 4 = Extremely):

3. I become tense if I have to talk about myself or my feelings.
4. I worry about expressing myself in case I appear awkward.
5. I find myself avoiding situations where I have to interact with peers.
6. I feel tense when I am with people I do not know well.
